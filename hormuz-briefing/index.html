01 — THE CURRENT PICTURE

A ceasefire that didn't end the leverage.

Transit has resumed, but not on pre-crisis terms. Two days after the MOU was signed, Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority circulated a document requiring that vessels transiting Hormuz obtain its own mandatory insurance — described in the session as, in effect, "my way or the highway." The panel was direct about what this signals: Hormuz remains Iran's ultimate bargaining chip, ceasefire or not.

Vessel traffic data offers a more encouraging read: vessels recently entering the Gulf outnumbered those leaving by more than two to one.

"The penny's dropped, big time — Hormuz is their ultimate bargaining chip."

ED TURNER · MECO GROUP

The first vessels to transit after the crisis broke were notably not the newest or best-insured: the panel noted many were over 20 years old, carrying no or IG cover, and possibly part of the sanctioned "shadow fleet" — the risk was taken first by those with the least to lose.

02 — TWO MARKETS, TWO OUTCOMES

Oil bent. Fertiliser broke.

The Strait is only 21 miles wide, but carries roughly a quarter of the world's seaborne oil and a fifth of its LNG. Even so, crude prices peaked under $110 — well short of the $150–200 some had feared — because the market had somewhere to go: four pipeline routes (albeit only two with real spare capacity) and strategic reserves, released to the tune of 400 million barrels. Traders are able to hedge to provide a degree of protection from extremes of price volatility, although with increasing imperfections.

<50% US strategic reserve now sits below half capacity — close to the practical minimum
$10–20m swing in value on a single 1–2m barrel cargo from $10 of price volatility alone
65–80 days of reserve buffer left at current drawdown rates

CATHERINE JAGO · CJH EXPERTS

Fertiliser had none of that flexibility. Most contracts are long term, there is no pipeline alternative and no liquid market to use for hedging products like urea and sulphur — so the shock landed directly on price. Sulphur was hit three ways at once: direct supply disruption, refineries lacking the crude to produce it as a byproduct, and a wave of export bans from Turkey, Vietnam, China and Russia.

"Sulphur usually trades at around $100 a tonne. At its peak this month, we saw it almost at $1,500 a tonne."

DHRUV PURI · AMEROPA

With no way to hedge the exposure, traders facing long-term supply obligations made a striking commercial choice: keep loading cargo from inside the Gulf, and instead price two months of war-risk cost directly into the contract — separating the payment mechanism from the physical shipping risk rather than walking away from deals built over years.

03 — HIGH PROFILE DISPUTES

Mercuria v The Baltic Exchange.

The Baltic Exchange's TD3C benchmark — the reference rate against which most of the crude freight market prices are set — is now under direct legal challenge. Mercuria's case, in outline: with the underlying Ras Tanura-to-China route effectively unusable, the benchmark stopped reflecting a real market, yet (Mercuria says) the Exchange took seven weeks to even consider revising it, despite having changed a comparable benchmark, also based on a Middle Eastern route, within a week.

The Baltic Exchange's defence is put forward on two bases: limitation and substantive defences to the claim. It argues that the UK's Benchmark Regulation confers no private right of action (enforcement sits exclusively with the FCA), and that in any event it acted with reasonable care and skill, citing that 55% of the market it consulted voted against changing the methodology.

The panel and audience members drew a direct parallel to LME v Elliott — the 2022 nickel dispute in which $12bn of trades were cancelled and a judicial review subsequently failed. Mercuria has not taken that route, bringing a claim in the Financial List instead. The panel's own reading, informed by that precedent: courts are traditionally very reluctant to "reconstitute an entire market."

WHAT TO WATCH

The case is expedited for an October hearing. Whatever the outcome, it will be the first real test of benchmark liability since LME v Elliott — with direct relevance to anyone with exposure priced off an index, not just freight, as well as what may be the first judicial finding of whether the Strait of Hormuz was ever closed to lawful shipping.
