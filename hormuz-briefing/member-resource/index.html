War-risk & transit clauses in practice

Six drafting and compliance points that came directly out of the 29 June session — for anyone reviewing, negotiating or standing behind a charter party, sale contract or freight-linked instrument touching the Gulf right now.

01 — THE CLAUSE THAT ACTUALLY GOT TESTED

The word "may" is not neutral anymore.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: audit "may" language in war risk clauses before your next fixture

A tribunal has already held that detention becomes payable mid-passage — despite the war risk clause using the permissive word "may" rather than a mandatory formulation — on the basis that an implied term allocates that risk to the charterer as the party best placed to bear it.

The practical exposure: if your standard war risk clause still uses "may" without further definition, you cannot assume that wording protects the owner from a mid-voyage cost allocation dispute. Tribunals are willing to look past the literal permissiveness of the word to the commercial reality of who controls the decision to proceed.

"The proper party to have inherited the risk of delay is the charterer — and the poor beleaguered charterer."

ED TURNER · MECO GROUP

Drafting point: where the parties intend "may" to mean unfettered owner discretion with no implied cost-sharing, say so expressly. Silence is now foreseeably going to be read against the party who could have drafted more precisely — which in most fixtures is the owner's broker.

02 — WHO ACTUALLY BENEFITS FROM A DIVERSION

Build in a benefit-sharing mechanism, not just a cost one.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: diversion clauses should address upside, not only downside

The session raised a pointed observation: owners who diverted vessels to alternative discharge points such as Khor Fakkan or Fujairah rather than transiting Hormuz may, in some cases, have ended up commercially better off — better positioned to trade — than if the original passage had gone ahead. Most war risk and deviation clauses are drafted purely around cost recovery for the diverting party and say nothing about a benefit that accrues to them as a result.

Drafting point: consider an express term requiring disclosure (and, where appropriate, an equitable adjustment) where a party invoking a war risk diversion right subsequently derives a material trading benefit from the diversion itself. Absent that, disputes will increasingly turn on implied good faith arguments rather than clear contractual mechanics.

03 — THE INSURANCE TRAP

A ceasefire does not mean the compliance risk has cleared.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: treat any newly mandated local insurer as a sanctions screening event

Two days after the relevant MOU was signed, Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority began circulating a document requiring its own mandatory insurance for vessels transiting Hormuz — in the session's words, "my way or the highway." Separately, the panel noted that general sanctions relief of the kind referred to in the session (a General Licence-type instrument) does not extend automatically to IRGC-linked or otherwise separately designated entities, even where a broader ceasefire licence is in effect.

COMPLIANCE FLAG

Any newly introduced local insurer, intermediary or "authority" tied to strait access should be treated as a fresh sanctions-screening event, not folded into existing compliance sign-off. Ceasefire terms and sanctions relief instruments operate on separate legal tracks and should never be assumed to move together.

Drafting point: charter parties and sale contracts fixed for Gulf-adjacent trade should include an express warranty that neither party will procure or pay for insurance, tolls or intermediary services from an entity requiring separate sanctions clearance, with a clean walk-away right if such a requirement is imposed post-fixture.

04 — FRUSTRATION WITHOUT A FINISH LINE

Don't assume the doctrine gives you a clean exit.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: consider a deemed-frustration longstop in future Gulf-adjacent fixtures

Unlike the Russia–Ukraine conflict, which developed a broadly accepted 12-month marker for when contracts of that vintage could reasonably be treated as frustrated, no equivalent marker has emerged for Hormuz. That leaves genuine uncertainty for any party assessing whether — and when — a frustration argument becomes available.

Drafting point: rather than relying on the common law doctrine to develop its own marker over time, consider drafting an express "deemed frustration" longstop into new fixtures touching the region — a defined period of continued closure or restriction after which either party may treat the contract as discharged, without needing to establish frustration as a matter of general law.

05 — THE GAP IN STANDARD BLOCKADE WORDING

Your war risk clause was not written for a law-of-war blockade.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: check whether your standard form addresses blockade specifically, not just "war risk" generally

Standard BIMCO-style war risk wording is largely silent on blockades beyond a standard short-notice provision (in the region of 48–72 hours), and was not drafted with a formally declared law-of-war blockade in mind — a materially different legal and operational scenario from a generalised war risk designation.

Drafting point: where a fixture touches a region with credible blockade risk, a bespoke blockade clause — addressing notice periods, cost allocation, and the parties' respective rights on a law-of-war blockade specifically — is worth the additional negotiating time. Standard war risk wording should not be assumed to cover this scenario by default.

06 — WHAT MERCURIA V THE BALTIC EXCHANGE MEANS FOR YOUR PAPER

If you price off a benchmark, you have exposure here too.

PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY: build a benchmark-discontinuation fallback into freight and COA pricing

The dispute over the Baltic Exchange's TD3C benchmark turns on whether an index can remain valid once its underlying route becomes commercially unusable. Regardless of the outcome, the case is a reminder that benchmark-linked pricing carries a structural risk that most freight and COA contracts do not currently address in their own drafting — they simply assume the administrator will handle it.

Drafting point: for any instrument priced off a route-specific benchmark, consider a fallback mechanism — similar in spirit to the LIBOR-transition drafting many finance lawyers are now familiar with — that specifies what happens if the benchmark administrator delays, refuses to adjust, or the route becomes unrepresentative for a sustained period. Do not leave that entirely to the administrator's discretion.

WHERE THIS GOES NEXT

The case is expedited for an October hearing. LSLC will issue a follow-up member briefing once judgment is available, with a focus on what it settles — and what it deliberately leaves open — for anyone drafting benchmark-linked freight paper.
